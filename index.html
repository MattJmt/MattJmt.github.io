About Me

Hi !

The Stillsuit assisting a user for walking.

A Comparative Study of Pulley and Bowden Transmissions in a Novel Cable-Driven Exosuit, the Stillsuit

Under the supervision of Prof. Robert Riener for my Master's thesis, I built the Stillsuit, a novel lower-limb soft exoskeleton with a pulley-based transmission, and coded in Python its associated hierarchical control. The pulley transmission outperformed the commonly-used Bowden cable efficiency by an additional 18.77% (steel cable) and 40.60% (Dyneema cable). The Stillsuit sets a new benchmark with 87.56% transmission efficiency, generating similar biological torques to those in literature while using smaller motors and a lighter actuation unit (1.92 kg).

Paper accepted to ICRA 2025. [GitHub]

Design and Control of an Ambidextrous Musculoskeletal Robotic Hand via Reinforcement Learning and Teleoperation

My team and I built a novel cable-driven robotic hand, featuring a four degree-of-freedom thumb to enhance dexterity and manipulation capabilities. The hand’s mechanical structure consisted of rolling contact and pin joints, high-friction silicone skin and a 3D-printed concave palm. We trained a policy to rotate a sphere in IsaacGym via reinforcement learning which successfully worked in real-life, while also using teleoperation to handle and grasp objects.

A Wearable IMU Device to model Patient Gait for Multiple Sclerosis

In the context of monitoring gait patterns for Multiple Sclerosis (MS) patients to determine their level of fatigue and the progression of the disease, I developed a wearable device that extracts step count and step period (cadence) from its user, and applies forward kinematics to the filtered IMU acceleration signals to generate a 3D model of the user.

Medical sensor to determine a mechanical property via mechanical vibration.

Ottobock Research Internship developing Medical Wearable Biomechanical Sensors

I developed three wearable sensors (one based on the DE102019121254A1 patent) which were successful at measuring human mechanical properties. One of the methods was: a) the human body part is excited to produce a mechanical vibration, b) the vibration is detected by a detection device, c) the detected vibration is evaluated by at least one electronic data processing device.
I coded motor control in C, interfaced sensors and electronic components, modelled hardware in CAD for 3D printing, and analysed signals in Python (FFT).

I cannot provide further details due to confidentiality.

The device was presented at the Great Exhibition Road Festival.

A Machine Vision Collision Avoidance Control Demonstrator

With the goal to educate 11-14 year-olds on the benefits of control systems for repetitive and time-critical tasks, this arcade-like device is increasingly automated in its task to avoid incoming obstacles. Its 3 modes are:
- Fully manual continuous joystick control
- Assisted discretised joystick control
- Automated Machine Vision control
Awarded highest grade in my cohort (81.8%) and showcased at the 2023 Great Exhibition Road Festival in London.

Buggy car navigating three different unseen mazes by detecting flashcards

Autonomous Robot to Navigate an Unseen Maze.

As part of my "Embedded C for Microcontrollers" class, the task was to code a small buggy robot car to navigate three increasingly complex unseen mazes by detecting coloured flashcards. Once reaching the final white card, the buggy had to retrace its steps to its starting position. My buggy successfully completed all three mazes, being the fastest to do so in my class.

Cam-piston mechanism, torque calculations, Ossur cheetah knee.

A Transfemoral Myoelectric Leg Prosthesis

With the objective to take part in the quadrennial Cybathlon competition, I led a team of 15 students at the ICL Prosthetics Society to build a hybrid passive - active prosthesis, acting freely in stance phase, and becoming motor-actuated when climbing stairs. Working with our pilot user, we devised a cam-piston mechanism to actuate Ossur's passive four-bar Cheetah knee, with IMUs to detect the change from passive to active mode. The team continued this project after my departure to ETH Zürich.

Literature review on the different bio-inspired underwater locomotion mechanisms.

A review on the Locomotion Mechanisms of Bio-inspired Aquatic Robots

This literature review addresses the various existing bio-inspired locomotion mechanisms present in aquatic robots and analyses the thrust generation model of the presented propulsion methods. Bio-inspired locomotion is more manoeuvrable, efficient, and less noisy than its propeller counter-part. With the technological improvements in soft materials, control and motor efficiency, bio-inspired robots have a bright future.

A Gravity Powered Light

Design and assembly of a Gravity Powered Light, intended to replace kerosene lights in developing countries. A suspended weight, as it falls, rotates a transmission to power a generator, feeding enery to the light bulb. Our lamp was able to shine for 7min47s with a 10kg falling weight.

Technical Presentation on Bionic Prostheses.

I delivered a 10-minute technical presentation on the differences between bionic(active) and passive prostheses. I discussed the gait cycle, dynamics, transmission and mechatronics involved in bionic prostheses.

This work was awarded the highest grade in my cohort (92%) and the Neil Watson Memorial Prize.

ZEPHAERO, a Start-up to improve the Aerodynamics of Freight Trains

When COVID-19 hit in March 2020, a friend and I sought to create a startup, Zephaero (“Wind” + “Aero”), to develop a novel mechatronic system capable of reducing the drag effect generated by inter-carriage gaps in freight trains. On top of using CAD and CFD softwares, this project involved investigating legal and IP setup, interviewing key market players, and establishing a business plan.
